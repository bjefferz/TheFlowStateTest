What Are PFAS? Understanding Forever
Chemicals, Regulations and More

What are PFAS? PFAS, or per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances, are a large group of widely used,
human-made chemicals. Commonly referred to as “forever chemicals”, they are extremely resistant
to breaking down in the environment and in the human body. This persistence leads to long-term
buildup and accumulation over time. PFAS are used in a wide range of consumer products, as well as
in industrial and manufacturing processes, primarily for their ability to repel water, oil, grease, and
stains. These chemicals can also travel through the environment, meaning direct contact with a
contamination source is not required for exposure. PFAS can move through soil, water, and air,
contributing to widespread global contamination and making cleanup efforts complex and costly.

What Should We Know About PFAS?

PFAS are found worldwide, and most people have been exposed to at least low levels of these
chemicals. Current research suggests that exposure to certain types or higher concentrations of
PFAS may be linked to various health concerns, including increased cholesterol, high blood
pressure, weakened immune functions, reduced fertility, and other potential effects. Ongoing
studies continue to examine how different PFAS compounds affect the body, how exposure levels
correlate with health outcomes, and what long-term, low-level exposure may mean for individuals
over time. Scientists are still working to better understand the full scope of health risks associated
with these persistent chemicals.

What can we do?

Although completely eliminating PFAS exposure is unlikely, individuals can take meaningful steps to
reduce personal risk. Contaminated drinking water is currently considered one of the most significant
exposure pathways. To better understand your own risk, consider testing your water. Local health
departments, water utilities, or environmental protection agencies can provide certified testing kits
that allow you to collect samples and send them to an accredited laboratory. Because PFAS require a
highly sensitive analytical method—liquid chromatography–tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS/MS)
—simple at home test kits cannot accurately detect them. While awaiting results, or as a general
precaution, using a water filtration system can help. Activated carbon filters and reverse osmosis
systems are the most effective at reducing PFAS levels, and several water pitchers are specifically
designed to filter PFAS. In addition, reducing your use of PFAS-containing products can help limit
exposure. Avoid items containing PTFE or other fluorinated chemicals, and consider choosing long-
lasting, reusable products instead of disposable ones.
